Turritella is a genus of medium-sized sea snails with an operculum, marine gastropod mollusks in the family Turritellidae.
They have tightly coiled shells, whose overall shape is basically that of an elongated cone.
The name Turritella comes from the Latin word turritus meaning "turreted" or "towered" and the diminutive suffix -ella.

Turritella ventricosa is an extinct species of sea snail, a marine gastropod from the family Turritellidae,
identified by its specific fossil characteristics.
While the name suggests a connection to the Turritella genus, its classification and appearance are distinct.
Information on living specimens is limited, but its fossil remains are an important part of paleontology.

• Classification: Turritella ventricosa is a species within the genus Turritella, belonging to the family
Turritellidae and phylum Mollusca. It is considered an extinct species.

• Fossil features: As a fossil, its shell is characterized by its specific shape and size, which paleontologists
use to identify it. Like other fossils in its genus, its shell may be preserved as a mold within rock,
showing the external or internal imprint of the original shell.

• Ecological role: Turritella species, including T. ventricosa, are known to be suspension feeders and
play a role in marine ecosystems. They are often found in soft sediments,
which is how their fossils can be preserved in mudstone or other rock types.
