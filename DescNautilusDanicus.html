Nautilus danicus is the former name of an extinct species of nautiloid, which is now taxonomically known as Hercoglossa danica.
This organism is a significant index fossil used for identifying the Danian age (earliest Paleocene epoch) in geological strata,
particularly in places like the Ninniyur beds in India and parts of Denmark.

Key facts about this species:
• Scientific Name: Hercoglossa danica (Schlotheim, 1820).
The original name given by Schlotheim was Nautilus danicus.
• Classification: It belongs to the genus Hercoglossa and the family Hercoglossidae.

• Geological Period: It is a fossil species, dating from the Maastrichtian (Late Cretaceous) to the Danian.
• (early Paleocene) periods. Its presence helps define the boundary between the Cretaceous and Paleogene periods in some regions.

• Description: Like modern nautiluses, it had a coiled, chambered shell.
Features: The suture is simple with broadly curved lobe and saddle.
Hercoglossa danica is one of many extinct shelled cephalopods, a group that was very diverse in ancient seas.

Fossil records indicate that nautiloids have experienced minimal morphological changes over the past 500 million years.
Many were initially straight-shelled, as in the extinct genus Lituites. They developed in the Late Cambrian period and
became a significant group of sea predators during the Ordovician period. Certain species reached over 2.5 m (8 ft) in size.

The other extant cephalopod subclass, Coleoidea, diverged from the nautiloids long ago and the nautilus has remained
relatively unchanged since. Nautiloids were much more extensive and varied 200 million years ago.
The ancestors of all Coleoidea (shell-less Cephalopods) once possessed shells, and many early cephalopod species are
only known from shell remains. Following the K-Pg extinction event most nautiloid species went extinct,
while members of Coleoidea managed to survive.
Following the mass extinction, the nautilus became the only extant species of nautiloids.
