Species: Mammites conciliatum (Stoliczka, 1865)

Mammites conciliatum (Stoliczka, 1865) is a species of extinct ammonite, a type of marine cephalopod mollusk
with a shell, which serves as a key index fossil for the Early Turonian age of the Late Cretaceous period.
Classification and Significance:
Scientific Name: Pseudaspidoceras (Ambakabites) conciliatum (Stoliczka, 1865).
It was originally described by Ferdinand Stoliczka in his work on the Cretaceous fauna of Southern India.

Group: Ammonite (a subclass of the Mollusca).
Time Period: It lived during the Early Turonian stage of the Late Cretaceous, approximately 93.9 to 89.8 million years ago.

Biostratigraphy:
This species is a significant index fossil, meaning its presence helps geologists determine the
precise age of the rock layers in which it is found.
It is associated with specific bio stratigraphic zones, such as the Mammites conciliatum Zone or the Mammites nodosoides Zone,
which are used to correlate rock sequences globally.

Physical Characteristics (as a fossil):
Ammonite fossils of this species typically exhibit features characteristic of the family Acanthoceratidae,
including a coiled shell (conch).
Ribs and potentially tubercles (knobs or spines) on the shell surface.
A specific suture pattern (the lines where internal walls meet the outer shell), which is used by paleontologists for identification.
