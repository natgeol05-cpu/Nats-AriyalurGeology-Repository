Peroniceras dravidicum (Kossmat, 1895) is an extinct species of ammonite, a type of shelled cephalopod,
belonging to the family Collignoniceratidae.
It is a significant index fossil used for dating geological layers to the early Coniacian stage of
the Late Cretaceous period.

Key Information:
• Scientific Name: Peroniceras dravidicum (Kossmat, 1895).
• Classification: It belongs to the Order Ammonitida, Superfamily Acanthoceratoidea, and Family Collignoniceratidae.
• Time Period: Coniacian stage (Upper Cretaceous).

• Geographical Distribution:
Fossils have been found in various parts of the world, including India (Tamil Nadu, the type locality)
North, West, and Central Africa, Madagascar, Italy, Zululand (South Africa), Utah, USA

Description:
The species is known for its distinct shell morphology, which includes specific patterns of ribbing and
tubercles (small knobs) on its shell.
Several other species described by Van Hoepen in the mid-20th century (P. spathi, P. platycostatum, P. multinodatum,
P. bessiae, P. obliquenodatum) are now considered synonyms or variations of P. dravidicum. (P = Peroniceras).

• Original Locality:
The type specimens were originally described from Uchaux, Vaucluse, in France.
• Geographic Distribution: The species has been found in Europe (including France and the Moesian Platform)
and is also recorded from other regions.
• Synonyms: It is known to have several important synonyms, indicating a degree of intraspecific variability.
These include Romaniceras (Romaniceras) uchauxiense and Romaniceras (Romaniceras) medlicottianum.

Biostratigraphy:
The presence (or absence) of P. dravidicum helps correlate rock layers internationally,
aiding in the establishment of global chronostratigraphy for the Late Cretaceous.
The Peroniceras dravidicum zone is an important biostratigraphic marker in the Coniacian of regions like Madagascar and India.

Research:
Ongoing research, such as the studies linked in the search results from ResearchGate and the University of Portsmouth,
continues to analyze the species and its associated fauna to refine our understanding of the Coniacian biostratigraphy and paleoecology.
