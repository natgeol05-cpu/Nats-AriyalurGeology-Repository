Romaniceras deverianum is the type species for the genus Romaniceras.

Romaniceras deverianum (d'Orbigny) is a species of ammonite that serves as an important fossil for dating geological periods.
It is known from the Turonian stage of the Late Cretaceous period. The species was first described by the paleontologist
Alcide d'Orbigny in 1841.

Overview • Scientific Name: Romaniceras deverianum (d'Orbigny, 1841).
• Taxonomic Classification: It is an ammonite belonging to the family Acanthoceratidae.
• Geologic Period: Late Cretaceous, specifically serving as an index fossil for the Middle to Upper Turonian stage.

• Distinguishing Characteristics:
Romaniceras deverianum has an evolute, or loosely coiled, shell.
The whorl section is typically round or oval in shape.
The shell features strong ribs and multiple rows of tubercles (small knobs).
Special Feature: An ammonite with 9 prominent tubercles in a row from this umbilicus to other side on long ribs.

• Original Locality:
The type specimens were originally described from Uchaux, Vaucluse, in France.
• Geographic Distribution: The species has been found in Europe (including France and the Moesian Platform)
and is also recorded from other regions.
• Synonyms: It is known to have several important synonyms, indicating a degree of intraspecific variability.
These include Romaniceras (Romaniceras) uchauxiense and Romaniceras (Romaniceras) medlicottianum.

Significance in paleontology
• Biostratigraphy:
Because its fossils appear in a relatively narrow and well-defined time frame,
Romaniceras deverianum is a valuable index fossil. It helps geologists and paleontologists correlate rock layers
and establish the precise age of rock sequences from the Turonian stage.

• Evolutionary Relationships:
The genus Romaniceras is believed to have evolved from an earlier ammonite genus, Kamerunoceras.
The study of R. deverianum and its close relatives provides insights into the evolution of Cretaceous ammonites.
• Ontogenetic and Intraspecific Variation: Research has shown that R. deverianum exhibits significant variation in its
shell features, both as it grew (ontogenetic variability) and among different individuals of the same species (intraspecific variability).
This is a notable characteristic within the Acanthoceratidae family.
