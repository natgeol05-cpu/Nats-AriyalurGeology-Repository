Description

Name: Eubaculites vaginia
Formation: Ariyalur Group
Locality: Ottakkovil in TANCEM mines
Classification: Molluscs: Cephalopoda:Ammonoidea: Ammonitida: SubOrder - Ancyloceratina, Family - Baculitidae

Eubaculites is an extinct genus of cephalopods in the family Baculitidae and each known species was initially
placed within the related genus Baculites until it was placed in a separate genus in 1926.
Eubaculites existed from the Turonian until the Danian, and is one of the very last genera of ammonites,
going extinct roughly 64.5 million years ago, which was around 500,000 years after the start of the Cenozoic.
Specimens found in the Maastricht Formation in The Netherlands suggest that at least one species
(E. carinatus) survived the K-Pg mass extinction event, albeit being restricted to the Danian.

A Baculite segment is a fossilized piece of the shell from a Baculites, an extinct genus of cephalopod
that lived during the Late Cretaceous period.
These fossils are known for their nearly straight, horn-like shells, and segments are often collected from
locations like the Pierre Shale in South Dakota or the Bearpaw Formation in Montana.
Some fossilized segments show iridescent colors due to opal replacement (ammolite) and may be found with other fossils.

• What it is: A piece of a Baculites fossil, which was a type of ammonite that had a straight shell,
unlike the more common coiled ammonites.
• Time period: The Baculites genus lived throughout most of the Late Cretaceous, approximately 75 to 80 million years ago.
• Fossilization: The fossils are often found preserved in shale or a concretion matrix.
• Appearance: Some segments can have a beautiful opalescent quality, known as ammolite,
with iridescent colors like pinks and greens when light hits them.
• Where they are found: They are common fossils in places like the Pierre Shale formation in South Dakota
and the Bearpaw Formation in Montana.
• Identification: The intricate internal structures called sutures are often used to identify the different
species within the Baculites genus.

Baculites showing sutures.
Baculites fossils are very brittle and almost always break.
They are most commonly found broken in half or several pieces, usually along suture lines.
Individual chambers found this way are sometimes referred to as "stone buffaloes" (due to their shapes).
